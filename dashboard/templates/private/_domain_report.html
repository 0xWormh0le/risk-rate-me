Can we trust that email communications are encrypted, private and secure?

95% of all hacking attacks and data breaches involve email. Yet critically more than 67% of organisations have not fully implemented readily available email authentication solutions.

Businesses can help stop phishing and protect their brands, customers and partners by using low cost, readily available email authentication solutions. These authentication solutions help to:

  • Prevent phishing on employees, customers and partners of your organisation
  • Prevent brand abuse & scams
  • Prevent Malware and Ransomware attacks
  • Protect employees from spear phishing, Invoice and CEO fraud

In addition to these authentication solutions Email Encryption provides additional email security. Encryption in transit helps protect your emails from being snooped on while they travel between you and your intended recipients. Without encryption they present a prime target for eavesdropping and mass interception as they cross dozens of optical fibres and routers. Without encryption your messages are as open to snoopers as a postcard in the mail.

Weak email security threatens your organisation and fails to protect the privacy and security of everyone in your digial ecosystem.

Can we trust the website to be encrypted protecting the privacy and security of visitors?

Sites that don’t use HTTPS judiciously are crippling the privacy controls you thought were protecting your data.

Our communications travel across a complex network of networks in order to get from point A to point B. Throughout that journey they are susceptible to interception by unintended recipients who know how to manipulate the networks.

Unencrypted websites can be used to do more than just steal sensitive information that a user might submit through their browser. An attacker can take advantage of the lack of encryption to inject malware into a website, which can lead to the complete compromise of a user’s computer and all of their data.

When properly configured, an HTTPS connection guarantees three things:

  • Confidentiality. The visitor’s connection is encrypted, obscuring URLs, cookies, and other sensitive metadata.
  • Authenticity. The visitor is talking to the “real” website, and not to an impersonator or through a “man-in-the-middle”.
  • Integrity. The data sent between the visitor and the website has not been tampered with or modified.

A plain HTTP connection can be easily monitored, modified, and impersonated. Whilst attacks against HTTPS connections are possible they are very difficult and require significant expense.

Can we trust that the website is protected from common vulnerabilities??

HTTP security headers are a fundamental part of website security. Upon implementation, they protect you against the types of attacks that your site is most likely to come across. These headers protect against cross-site scripting (XSS), man-in-the-middle (MitM) attacks, clickjacking, cross-site request forgery and other threats.

These threats are among those listed in the "Ten Most Critical Web Application Security Risks" published by The Open Web Application Security Project (OWASP). Security headers are essential in helping to protect your site, and your visitors from easily preventable vulnerabilities.

Cookies can contain sensitive data such as authentication information. These are sent over an HTTP/HTTPS connection and are stored in web browsers as plain text, and so can be targeted and be used by attackers to steal the information stored in it. To prevent such information exposure cookies are need to be secured.
